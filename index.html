Servus in Congregatione

Assume sex between men is immoral

The Christian congregation had slaves. In the Roman Empire, slaves were property used for sexual gratification by masters. Were these men—used for sex by masters—denied baptism? Would God be partial against them for their circumstances? (Acts 10:34-35).

Paul encouraged slaves not to run away (1 Cor. 7:21–24), effectively instructing them to submit to their masters. If a slave was used for sex, could he be removed? "Neither height nor depth nor any other creation can separate us from God’s love." The master is part of creation; he cannot separate the slave from the Kingdom. Thus, some in the congregation had sex with men with impunity.

Does slavery bestow a righteousness on lovers not found in the relationship of two free men? Where is the line drawn?

Romans 8:38–39  •  1 Corinthians 7:21–24  •  Acts 10:34

Tres Genera Eunuchorum

Matthew 19:12 • The Master Illustrator

Jesus identified three groups: those born eunuchs, those made eunuchs by men (often for sexual purposes), and those who choose it for the Kingdom. The second group were not celibate; they were a category created for sex with men. Jesus mentioned them before saying some would choose to be eunuchs for the Kingdom.

What was the illustrative purpose of including this sexual group if their lifestyle was to be avoided? This was the moment to add a limiting commandment. None was added. Instead: "Let the one who can make room for it make room for it."

The generalization is that some do not marry, with the option of either celibacy or sex with men, following Jesus's commandments and remaining loved by him.

Matthew 19:12  •  Deuteronomy 23:1  •  Acts 8:27

Dilectus Meus Mihi

2 Samuel 1:26 • The Greatest Romance

David and Jonathan's love was "more wonderful than the love of women." This was not just a comparison of intensity, but of nature. It was sexual and more. They risked lives to hug and kiss (1 Sam 20:41). Jonathan's love for David is the biblical example of "loving someone as yourself."

The Bible notes David did not have sex with Abishag (1 Kings 1:4) because it affected legal rights. It makes no such note regarding Jonathan because there was no law to violate. In the camp of soldiers, "seeing each other's nakedness" was a bonding value. Jehovah constructed the Law to regulate emissions (Lev. 15), but never once prohibited the love between these two men whom He approved.

2 Samuel 1:26  •  1 Samuel 20:41  •  1 Kings 1:4  •  1 Samuel 23:16–18

Lex et Porneia

Redefining the Burden

If there were regulations against two men masturbating each other, they would be as clear as the regulations for wet dreams or animal sacrifice. None exist. God did not make a mistake by omitting them.
