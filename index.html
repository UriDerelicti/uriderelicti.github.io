Axiom: "All Scripture is inspired of God and beneficial ... for setting things straight ... so that the man of God may be fully competent, completely equipped" - 2 Timothy 3:16, 17.

Line of Reasoning: If no other conclusion can survive the argument, then it must be the correct conclusion.

Assume sex between men is immoral. The Christian congregation had slaves. The slaves in the Roman empire were property and could be used for sexual gratification by their masters and their masters' friends. Were male slaves used by their masters for sexual gratification not allowed to be part of the congregation? Were they not baptised by Holy Spirit in the first century? Would that not be partial against someone due to his circumstances rather than his heart? Because God is not partial.

What if the male slave never had sex with another man and the slave came into the Christian congregation. If he was used, as a slave, for sex by another man, would he be removed from the congregation? Neither height nor depth nor any other creation can separate us from God's love. The master is part of God's creation, So how could he separate the slave from God's love? and the congregation? and the Kingdom?

Male slaves were encouraged to not run away; therefore, to submit to homosexuality if asked of them by their master. Therefore some men in the Christian congregation had sex with other men, with impunity.

Assume then that the restriction on men having sex with men was narrower than an absolute prohibition. Where was the line drawn then? Was the sex allowed by virtue of the man being a slave? Because a slave could very much have been loved by his master, as the Centurion loved his boy friend. There was nothing legally preventing the Centurion from making love with him. The slave could very much enjoy and want a sexual relationship with his master; certainly some slaves were homosexual in nature. Does then slavery bestow on two wanting lovers a righteousness not found in the loving relationship of two men that does not include slavery?

Jesus provided the illustration of the men who would choose to be eunuchs for the Kingdom. He compared them to men born eunuchs (who may not have had a sexual appetite or ability to have procreative sex with a woman and thus remained unmarried). But Jesus also included a second group- those who were made eunuchs by men. These men were castrated or crushed in the testicles outside of the Roman empire (as it was illegal in Rome) and imported with high tariffs for sexual purposes. These men were created as a group for sex; they were not celibate. Jesus said some men chose to be eunuchs for the Kingdom after referencing celibate and homosexual eunuchs. What was the illustrative purpose for including this second, sexual group? Surely the master illustrator was not simply yapping. Why did Jesus say some would choose to be eunuchs after mentioning eunuchs that had sex with men if men were to NOT be like those Eunuchs but like the other eunuchs? This would have been the time to add a commandment prohibiting all or, as demonstrated, some homosexuality if any was to be avoided to be pleasing to God.

NB- Jesus was not suggesting the men were made eunuchs by men when they were appointed to secular offices, as if men should choose to administer the government's treasury for the Kingdom since men born eunuchs don't map to this point and he was answering a question regarding marriage. The answer was about marriage directly, and therefore penile vaginal sex, and also homosexuality and abstinence by virtue of using two groups of eunuchs (both of which could have been celibate or practicing homosexuals). Jesus blessed two men in love and unrestrained in their sex with each other when he healed the Centurion's boy friend.

Saying, "some men are twinks that can never marry and some will choose to be like them, for the Kingdom" implies some men righteously choosing a homosexual life without a marriage. So why did Jesus not give a clear commandment here or elsewhere against homosexuality when he referenced men specifically kept for sex, knowing that some men could only have their sexual needs satisfied by another man?

Instead, Jesus did say those following his commandments would be loved by him. The first century congregation did include male slaves, which could be used for sex at any point. Jesus added to his illustration a group of men used for sex with other men but did NOT add any commandment limiting homosexuality after he brought it up, instead, he said some men would chose to be like these eunuchs- in the congregation, loved by Jehovah, and having sex with men.

The generalization is not that some men would need to make themselves slaves of men for the Kingdom since men born eunuchs were not slaves as a group. Both men who were born eunuchs and men made eunuchs could (and did) have sex with men. The generalization is that some men do not marry with the option of either celibacy or sex with men. They were following Jesus's commandments, part of the congregation, and having sex with other men. And they were loved according to Jesus.

David and Jonathan

I believe that David and Jonathan had the greatest romance described in the scriptures. And they were both approved by Jehovah, so much so that He described Jesus as the second David. Jonathan chose David (according to Saul) and waited for him for years, planning to be by David's side when David ruled. They risked their lives to hug and kiss each other, after David hid out three days outside Gibeah. Jonathan couldn't even put in a full archery session for the ruse before abandoning it to hold David- who he loved as himself. In the NT, husbands are encouraged to cherish their wives; they could look to Jonathan as an example of loving someone as you do yourself. NB- David leaving his wife is also recorded, in stark contrast to his leaving Jonathan- details added to the record for a purpose.

Jonathan- knowing priests had been killed for giving the food to David on the run and that his father twice tried to kill him- traveled some distance to find David in the wilderness (which his father could not do) and encourage David. It was only after this restating their vows and they had been separated by about 5 years that David started having children. While they were together for about 2 years after Goliath was killed, they lived in the same city, regularly ate dinner at the king's table, and were deployed as soldiers in wars. Seemingly, Jonathan helped David- too young to serve in the militia- learn to command men. He would end up being the overseer of a thousand soldiers. During this time where the two were deployed together, I don't believe they would have risked being removed from the camp because of a wet dream, as the Law required, potentially keeping them from battle. Nor would they ejaculate with women or animals. Nor did they have privacy to be alone. Jehovah constructed the Law to remove the men separating themselves for their fellows sexually for the day and then having them clean up with the soldiers, "seeing each other nakedness"- a bonding that was valuable for soldiers fighting side by side.

I believe that the love between two men can be greater than the love between a man and a woman, like David said. David's words were that Jonathan's love was greater than the love of women - not that David loved Jonathan more than all of his wives and concubines, or even all women- but that Jonathan's love was greater than the love women can provide, which is largely sexual. It was "more than" because it was sexual and more. And the sexual aspect was not commented on because it was not a matter of law. If it was unlawful, it would have been stated one way or another if they had sex, the same as it was commented that he did not have sex with Abishag, which affected her legal rights to marry or be taken by the next king. That pattern was followed by Jonathan and David in that there was no comment one way or the other about sex between them as there was no law to violate, no wronged party to bring a case, no compensation to be had.
